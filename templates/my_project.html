Research Question

The price of tuition in the U.S. has remained fairly steep over the years. Between the 1983-84 and 2013-14 academic years, inflation-adjusted tuition increased by 153% at private four-year colleges and 231% at public four-year universities (College Board). Tuition costs play a major factor in deciding which college to attend, especially for students from low-income families. While private universities are traditionally thought to be more expensive with their high tuition, recent deep cuts in state funding for public institions has contributed to rising tuition among state universities. The average state is spending 20% less per student in 2015 compared to 2008 (Center on Budget and Policy Priorities). In this project, I examine the trends in tuition prices and post-undergraduate debt among low-income families between 4-year private (non-profit) and public universities, in the past two to three years. I seek to identify how much tuition fees have changed and how this compares to changes in grant/scholarship funding from institutional and state governments. I hope that my conclusions can shed light on the factors of increased tuition and fees, as well as provide insight on how college pricing varies between public and private universities.

Project Description

In this project, I analyzed college data available on the web between 2013-2015, through certain techniques used in conjunction with Python. The data collection process consisted of two methods:

  1. Webscraping: Through Python's BeautifulSoup library and Requests module, I was able to collect information on changes in tuition, and grants/scholarships, specifically, local and institutional grants/scholarships (this data is only available for full-time freshman undergraduates), between the 2013/14 and 2014/15 academic years, via the College Navigator website. The information is scraped from a search query containing pages of college links. Each link provides a table of financial and tuition information for the chosen college. I searched for fairly competitive 4-year public and private (non-profit) colleges with 5%-50% admission rates in the U.S. You may click on public and private to see what search results were produced from these two queries. My Python code iterates over all of the pages, then retrieves tuition, scholarships, and grants data from each of the table's cells. This information is then appended to a dictionary, which is printed out at the end.

  2. Using json API calls: Through Python's "requests" module, I was able to attain student debt information from College Scorecard's database, displayed in json format(Source: U.S. Department of Education), containing bulk data of thousands of public and private colleges. I was limited to information from 2013, as this year had more results (perhaps 2014 results have not all been published yet). My Python code called for College Scorecard's API with the field parameter limiting the results to either public or private (non-profit) colleges. With the data in JSON format, I then created a "for-loop", which appended information on median debt, paid by low-income students, to a dictionary. I performed this process for both private and public colleges.

Results- Public Colleges

Between the 2013-14 and 2014-15 academic school years, among public 4-year colleges with admission rates between 5%-50%, I was able to determine the following changes in tuition:

  • There were 87 four-year public colleges (admission between 5%-50%), of which, 74 universities increased their tuition between the 2013-14 and 2014-15 academic years.
  • The average in-state tuition increased by 4.04%.
    • Highest in-state tuition increase: Prairie View A&M University at 39.8%
    • Highest in-state tuition decrease: Eastern Illinois University at -0.3%
  • The average out-of-state tuition also increased by 4.04%.
    • Highest out-of-state tuition increase: Prairie View A&M University at 39.9%
    • Highest out-of-state tuition decrease: University of Memphis at -11.7%

Among the same colleges for the 2013-14 academic year, I determined the following statistics for beginning full-time undergraduates:

  • Average Annual Net Price was $11,496.
  • Overall, the average percentage of students with grants or scholarships was 73.76% and the average amount was $8,785
    • State/local grants or scholarships: the average percentage was 45.89% and average amount was $4,081
    • Institutional grants or scholarships: the average percentage was 42.58% and the average amount was $4,915

    For low-income students who graduated in 2013 (low-income is defined as having a family income of $30,001-$75,000), post-graduate median was $10,497, among all public universities (College Scorecard).

Results -Private Colleges

Between the 2013-14 and 2014-15 academic school years, among private (non-profit) 4-year colleges with admission rates between 5%-50%, I was able to determine the following changes in tuition:

  • There were 246 four-year private colleges (admission between 5%-50%), of which, 212 universities increased their tuition between the 2013-14 and 2014-15 academic years.
  • The average tuition increased by 2.91%
    • Highest tuition increase: New York School of Interior Design at 24.2%
    • Highest tuition decrease: Alaska Pacific University at -34.2%

Among the same colleges for the 2013-14 academic year, I determined the following statistics for beginning full-time undergraduates:

  • Average Annual Net Price was $22,307.
  • Overall, the average percentage of students with grants or scholarships was 79.03% and the average amount was $21,663
    • Institutional grants or scholarships: the average percentage was 73.43% and the average amount was $19,103

For low-income students who graduated in 2013 (low-income is defined as having a family income of $30,001-$75,000), post-graduate median debt was $18,423, among all private universities (College Scorecard).


Final Conclusions

Comparing the statistics for fairly competitive U.S. public and private (non-profit) colleges, we can conclude the following information:

  • Overall, despite recent state cuts in public university funding, private colleges still had a higher net price than public colleges in the 2013-14 academic year, by $10,811 (for full-time beginning undergraduates). Even more, for low-income students graduating in 2013, those attending private colleges had $7,926 more in debt.
  • At the same time, we see that 85% of public schools increased tuition costs between the 2013-14 and 2014-15 academic years, roughly the same rate as that of private schools, at 86%. Public schools also increased their tuition costs by 1.13% more than that of private schools between those two academic years.
    • Among all private and public colleges, public college tuition increases went up to 39%, while the highest private college tuition increase was 24%. However, private tuition decreased as much as -34% while public tuition decreased only to -12%. This implies that tuition increases are much steeper for public colleges, while tuition decreases are much steeper for private colleges, perhaps this allows private colleges to stay competitive and attract more students.
  • In terms of Grants/Scholarships overall, 5.27% more students at private colleges had grants/scholarships than at public colleges during the 2013-14 academic year. Furthermore, private colleges disbursed an average of $12,878 more than public colleges. In terms of grants/scholarships that are institutional-based, there were 30.85% more private students with institutional grants/scholarships, which were on average $14,118 higher than those disbursed by public colleges. This makes sense given the higher costs of private colleges, and perhaps more availability of funds, since private schools have a smaller student body.

It is interesting to note that while private colleges provide a higher amount of grants/scholarships to a larger percent of incoming beginning undergraduates, net tuition is still higher at private colleges. This trickles down to low-income students attending private colleges, as they graduate with almost $8,000 more in debt. Although public colleges have traditionally been viewed as less costly, the gap between public and private college tuition costs clearly is gradually getting narrower, as the percentage of public vs. private colleges with increasing tuition costs was approximately the same in the 2013-14 academic year. Further research could compare these statistics to 5 or even 10 years ago, in order to examine how this gap between public and private college, in terms of tuition, grant/scholarship disbursement, and student debt, have evolved over time.
